## Why this is a good conference talk

**The edge is the format itself.** Ten minutes, memorized, no slides needed — it fits
the slots keynotes can't: lightning tracks, unconference sessions, panel openers, the
"we have ten minutes before lunch" gap every conference has and never fills well. A
speaker with a great ten-minute talk gets invited back for the forty-minute one; this
is the portfolio's door-opener.

**The content edge:** the nine-minute "five principles for AI coding" genre is huge and
correct — and reliably missing enforcement, the team, evidence, and coherence. This
talk *is* that genre plus the missing strap, so it rides the audience's existing
familiarity instead of fighting it. The single most quotable beat — "personal
discipline is holding the leash; equipment works at 11 PM before the demo" — is built
for the hallway conversation afterward, which is what ten-minute talks are for.

**Rooms:** lightning tracks anywhere engineers gather; internal brown-bags and guild
sessions (its original habitat); AI-tooling meetups; as the opening act paired with
Talk 2 (this one says "put discipline in equipment," Talk 2 says "and here's the seam
the equipment can't cross" — they sequence perfectly as a half-day track).

**The change it aims for:** one team moves one principle from someone's head into a
gate that week. The talk literally assigns this in minute seven.

## Q&A drill (the ten-minute format gets three questions, so drill the big three)

**"Doesn't this slow us down? Speed is the whole point of AI coding."** The asymmetry
is the answer: generation is nearly free, verification is not — so the bottleneck was
never typing speed, and discipline at the verification bottleneck is the *only* speed
that compounds. Undisciplined speed is borrowing against the review queue. **"We tried
test-first and it didn't stick."** It didn't stick because it was held by willpower —
that's the talk's whole thesis. Wishes fail at 11 PM; gates don't. Don't retry the
habit; install the CI gate that makes the habit unnecessary. **"Where does the context
file live and who owns it?"** In the repo, owned by the team, PR-reviewed like code —
because context living in one person's prompts is context the team doesn't have. Ten
minutes of writing it saves the hours the agent spends rediscovering your 2023
mistakes with 2026 speed.
