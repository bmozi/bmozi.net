# Talk 8 · Fund Slices, Not Programs

*The transformation-economics talk — 25 minutes. Rooms: executive tracks, CIO/CTO
forums, transformation and portfolio-management conferences. Backed by Seams Part 10.
Views my own.*

---

## Why this is a good conference talk

**The edge is that it's a money talk wearing an architecture jacket.** Executive rooms
have heard "big bang bad, iterative good" for twenty years; what they haven't heard is
the *funding mechanics* — slices that measure their own effect against a counterfactual
and *earn* the next slice with evidence, populations that onboard to the new system so
the old one drains rather than migrates, and the kill-switch framing ("never one budget
cycle from worthless") that speaks fluent CFO. This is the rare architecture talk a
finance leader can act on without believing anything technical. It also travels: the
same mechanics work for platform replacements, ERP journeys, data programs, and AI
rollouts, so every attendee has a live program to apply it to.

**The change it aims for:** one leader re-scopes a program's next funding gate from
"phase 2 of the plan" to "the thinnest slice a customer would feel in ninety days."

## The talk (25 minutes — full script with minute marks)

**[Minutes 0–4 — the obituary]**
Every transformation has the same obituary, so let me read the composite version.
Three-year program. Nine-figure budget. Executive sponsor, steering committee, a
war room with a countdown clock. Dead at month eighteen — and here's the detail that
matters: not killed by bad technology. The platform worked. It died of its own mass —
sponsor turnover, budget season, one reorg — because of a structural property that was
visible on day one if anyone had looked for it: for its entire life, the program was
**one budget cycle from worthless**. Cancel it any quarter before the end, and you
keep nothing. No customer felt anything yet; the value was all loaded into a future
that never arrived. That's not a plan. That's a hostage situation with a steering
committee. And the fix is not "be more agile" — you've heard that talk. The fix is a
different *funding instrument*, and that's what the next twenty minutes are.

**[Minutes 4–10 — the slice, precisely]**
The unit of funding is a **thin vertical slice**, and I need to be precise, because
every program office in the world will tell you they already do this and almost none
do. A slice is not a layer — "first we build the data platform" is a layer, and a
customer will never feel a layer. A slice is not a phase — phases are the old plan
wearing agile clothing. A slice eliminates **one class of pain, end to end** — a
journey a real person completes on the new path, start to finish, in production. And
a slice carries two obligations the layer never had. It **measures its own effect
against a counterfactual**: what did this pain cost before — in hours, in churn, in
call volume — and what does it cost now? Baseline the week before; measure the month
after; publish the delta. And its evidence is the **application for the next slice's
funding**. Not the roadmap — the receipt. Funding follows receipts. Say that sentence
in your next portfolio review and watch who gets uncomfortable; the discomfort is
diagnostic.

**[Minutes 10–16 — drain, don't migrate]**
Now the scariest word in any transformation: migration. The big-bang cutover, the
weekend nobody sleeps, the rollback plan everyone prays isn't real. Here's the
pattern that deletes the word. **New populations onboard to the new system first.**
New customers, new cohorts, new regions — people with no history to move — start on
the new path, where the slices are. Existing populations stay put, and move only at
their natural moments, if ever. The old system doesn't get migrated. It **drains** —
population by population, workload by workload, until the day the last one leaves
and you turn it off having never performed a cutover at all. No frozen dual-write
purgatory as a permanent state; a visible finish line that gets closer every
quarter. Now the honest caveat, on stage, because a CFO in the second row is already
computing it: the drain has a **carrying cost**. Two systems run concurrently for
the duration, and that's real money. So the slice math must include the overlap —
each slice's counterfactual is charged its share of the double-running — and the
drain gets its own end date with a name attached, or "drain" quietly becomes
"forever," and forever is how you end up with the mainframe this conference has been
promising to retire since before some of you were hired.

**[Minutes 16–21 — the governance that makes it real]**
Three pieces of equipment keep this honest, because without them "slices" is just
smaller theater. First, **every slice ships with its instrument**: the metric, the
counterfactual, and the tripwire — the number that, if missed, stops the program and
triggers the review. A program that can't name what would kill it isn't governed;
it's *believed*, and belief is not a portfolio strategy. Second, **the target
architecture is a contract, not a promise**: slices build against the same
boundaries and standards, funded one at a time — so ten slices cohere into a system
rather than ten prototypes. The vision survives; only the *funding* of the vision
changed. Third, the **portfolio effect**, which is the real pitch to the money in
the room: five slices in, you are in one of exactly two positions. Either you hold
five receipts and deserved momentum — the program has *earned* its scale — or you
found out the thesis was wrong for the price of one slice. Both outcomes beat the
alternative, which is discovering the same thing at month thirty for the price of
the whole program. Slicing isn't slower. It's the only version where finding out
early is cheap.

**[Minutes 21–25 — the Monday test and the close]**
Two questions to take back to whatever program is nearest you. First: what is the
thinnest slice of this vision that a customer would *feel* in ninety days? Not a
milestone — a felt difference, in a real person's week. If the honest answer is
"nothing until year two," you've located the risk, and it isn't technical. Second
— the question I'd ask of every program in your portfolio this quarter: if this
were cancelled the quarter before completion, what would we keep? If the answer is
"nothing," you are not funding a transformation. You are funding an outcome lottery
with a very well-produced ticket. The programs that survive contact with budget
season are the ones that were never more than ninety days from their last receipt.
Fund slices. Demand counterfactuals. Let the old system drain. And keep the bold
picture — every word of it — purchased one proven slice at a time. The tools were
never the gap. The seams were — and the seam between the roadmap's story and the
ledger's evidence is the one this practice governs. Thank you.

## One-slide summary

Big programs die of their own mass — and spend most of their life **one budget cycle
from worthless**. **The slice:** one class of pain, eliminated end to end, felt by a
real person in ninety days. **The funding rule:** each slice measures itself against a
counterfactual; evidence is the application for the next slice. **The migration rule:**
new populations onboard first; the old system drains (with a priced overlap and an end
date). **The governance:** every slice carries its metric, counterfactual, and
tripwire. **Two questions:** thinnest slice felt in 90 days? Cancelled early — what do
you keep?

## Q&A drill

**"Some things can't be sliced — you can't half-replace a general ledger."** True, and
the pattern isn't half-replacement; it's population-based coexistence with a governed
seam between old and new. If even that is impossible, you've learned the program's real
risk class — price it as the bet it is, don't disguise it as phases. **"Slices produce
integration debt — ten thin things that don't cohere."** Only if the seams between
slices are ungoverned; the target architecture is the *contract* each slice builds
against, funded by evidence instead of faith. The alternative coherence — the big
bang's — costs three years to find out. **"Our board wants the big vision, not
increments."** Give them the vision *and* the funding rule: the horizon picture intact,
purchased one receipted slice at a time. Boards don't hate vision; they hate unpriced
optimism. **"Doesn't the counterfactual cost too much to measure?"** A baseline week of
measuring the pain before the slice ships is the cheapest insurance in the portfolio —
without it, the program's benefits are testimony, and testimony is what got the last
transformation funded.
