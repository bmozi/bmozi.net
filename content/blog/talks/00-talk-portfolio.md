# The Talk Portfolio

*The speaking program: which talk, for which room, making which argument. Every talk here
is generic by design — no employer names, no identifiable systems, composite examples —
and every one is downstream of written work that survived adversarial review first. The
house rule for the podium is the same as for the page: never assert on stage what the
corpus can't demonstrate in writing. Gate before anything is submitted anywhere: employer
policy review (social media, outside activity, IP) — same gate as publishing.*

---

## The portfolio at a glance

**Talk 1 · "The Tools Were Never the Gap"** — the flagship keynote. Systems thinking for
general engineering audiences: why organizations drowning in complaints already own
world-class tools, and why the failures live in the ungoverned seams between them. The
40-minute version of the entire Seams series. *Rooms:* general software conferences,
engineering leadership summits, internal all-hands. *Difficulty to deliver:* low — it's
storytelling with one framework. *Best first talk.*

**Talk 2 · "The Seam AI Review Can't Cross"** — the AI-engineering talk. Verification
didn't die when generation got cheap; it moved up a level, onto a seam no automated
reviewer can cross: whether the spec was true. Correlated verifiers, engineered
independence, and the owner of intent. *Rooms:* AI/ML engineering conferences, platform
engineering, QA/testing conferences (they will love it — it dignifies their whole field).
*Edge:* the correlated-verifier risk is documented in research and acknowledged by no
vendor — this talk says something the sponsored track won't.

**Talk 3 · "The Delegation Seam"** — the security/agentic talk. Authority is the most
dangerous thing systems move, agents move it in chains, and nobody attenuates. The
confused deputy at industrial scale, the chain's envelope as the ownable object, and the
fifth question: can this authority be attenuated and revoked mid-flight? *Rooms:*
security conferences, identity/IAM events, agentic-AI tracks. *Edge:* rides directly on
2025–2026 standards work (RFC 8693 chains, attenuating-token drafts, federated agent
protocols) — timely for exactly the next two years.

**Talk 4 · "Purpose on the Wire"** — the specialist/domain talk. What education (and most
industries) can steal from healthcare: the purpose-of-use discipline — why "who may
access what" is solved everywhere and "for what reason, on this request" is solved almost
nowhere outside FHIR. *Rooms:* higher-ed IT (EDUCAUSE-style), data governance and privacy
engineering conferences, interoperability events. *Edge:* concrete, checkable, and nobody
else is giving it.

**Talk 5 · "The Harness in Ten Minutes"** — already built, lives with the book (Reference
13). The AI-coding discipline talk: Scope, Prove, Enforce, Communicate; equipment over
willpower. *Rooms:* brown-bags, guild sessions, lightning tracks. The 10-minute format is
the point — it fits where keynotes don't.

## The second shelf (written — full kits, Talks 6–9)

**Talk 6 · "Prosecute Your Own Architecture"** — the engineering-culture talk. The edge
is receipts: the speaker arrives with a published record of arguing against his own
designs, convictions included — credibility *is* the content. Staff-plus and
architecture tracks. **Talk 7 · "The Org Chart Is Equipment Too"** — the leadership
talk. The edge is agreeing with the AI-team-shrinking hype and completing it: the
three-question Monday test aimed at the reorg proposal already in every attendee's
inbox. Director/VP tracks. **Talk 8 · "Fund Slices, Not Programs"** — the executive
talk. The edge is funding mechanics in fluent CFO: slices that earn the next slice
with evidence, drain-don't-migrate, and "never one budget cycle from worthless."
CIO/CTO forums. **Talk 9 · "No Queue Without an Owner"** — the ops talk. The edge is
universality plus a napkin-simple invariant, with the agent-era extension (the owner
owns the behavioral envelope) that even jaded ops rooms haven't heard. SRE and
service-management conferences.

## Portfolio mechanics

One talk per quarter, maximum, while the day job is new — the shedding order already
covers this (publishing sheds first). Every talk ends with the same signature line the
series does, building recognition across rooms: *the tools were never the gap; the seams
were.* Slides stay near-empty (one claim per slide, the one-slide summary as the leave-
behind); the talk kit format below carries: why this talk exists, the audience and the
room it's for, the full spoken script with minute marks, the one-slide summary, and the
Q&A hard-question drill — because the Q&A is where talks are actually won, and the
adversarial-review habit means the hard questions are already written down.
