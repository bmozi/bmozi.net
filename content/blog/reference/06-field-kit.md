# The Days 1–30 Field Kit

*The listening instrument. Every conversation in the first thirty days produces structured evidence for the seam audit instead of impressions — without ever feeling like an interrogation to the person across the table. Principles under load: **Earn Trust** (listen genuinely), **Learning** (inquiry before conclusions), **Student Obsessed** (every question eventually traces to a student).*

---

## Rules of conduct (read before every session)

Talk less than a third of the time. You are not there to preview conclusions — people can tell when questions are auditions for answers you've already reached. Never solution in the room; "that's exactly the kind of thing I'm here to understand" is a complete sentence. Verify by saying back: "So if I heard you right…" — the correction you get is often the real finding. Always end with the servant question: *"What's in your way that someone like me could move?"* — and if you promise to move something, log it and move it. Your first thirty days of kept micro-promises are worth more than any presentation.

One more: write the verbatim gems. When someone says something in words sharper than yours — "we find out about start-date changes when the student calls crying" — capture it exactly. Their words carry authority in later rooms that your paraphrase never will.

## The universal opening set (any role)

1. "Walk me through your best day and your worst day in the last month — what made each one that way?"
2. "When something goes wrong for a student in your area, how do you find out?" *(listen for: the student told us / a report / an alert / we don't)*
3. "What's a question you need answered every week that takes longer to answer than it should?"
4. "If you could make one other team's system tell you one thing automatically, what would it be?" *(this question IS the seam audit, in disguise)*
5. "What has been tried before that didn't stick — and what's your theory of why?"

## Role-specific sets

**Registrar & records** *(their currency: record integrity and risk reduction)*
- "What change to a student record would you most want to be impossible to make silently?"
- "When there's a dispute about what a record said on a past date, how do you resolve it today?"
- "Which parts of the record do you own outright, and which do you share custody of — and where does shared custody hurt?"
- "What would you need to see, over how long, before trusting a new system with any part of the official record?"
- Listen for: evaluation queues and their owners, correction workflows, conferral timing, the words they use for holds and statuses (vocabulary collisions live here).

**Enrollment leadership & counselors** *(currency: fewer stalled students, cleaner handoffs)*
- "From application to first day, where do students go quiet on you — and where do you go quiet on them?"
- "When a file stalls, who notices first: your team, or the student?"
- "What do you have to look up in more than one system to answer one student's question?"
- Listen for: the black-hole zones, the handoff to aid and records, what "committed" means in their vocabulary vs. everyone else's.

**Financial aid** *(currency: compliance safety plus student clarity)*
- "Which aid-status changes do students most often learn about later than they should?"
- "Where does regulation force your process shape, and where is the shape just history?"
- "What does the rest of the institution misunderstand about what you can and can't share?"
- Listen for: Title IV boundaries (buy-never-build territory), the aid↔records↔enrollment triangle, purpose-limitation instincts they already have.

**Program mentors** *(currency: time back for students, earlier warning)*
- "What do you spend time chasing that the system should just tell you?"
- "How do you find out a student is in trouble — and how late is that, usually?"
- "What promise have you made to a student that the systems made hard to keep?"
- Listen for: the 360-view gaps, commitment tracking, momentum signals they infer manually today.

**Instructors & evaluators leadership** *(currency: signal quality, fairness protection)*
- "What would readiness look like if you could see it before a student attempts an assessment?"
- "Where does the evaluation process protect fairness well, and where does it merely protect procedure?"
- Listen for: the assessment lifecycle in their words — this is competency-ledger domain language arriving from its true owners.

**Platform & data engineers** *(currency: fewer 2am pages, less archaeology)*
- "What integration do you own that you'd least like to explain to a new hire?"
- "Where do two systems disagree about the same fact, and who wins today?"
- "What breaks when [the biggest system] has a bad night?"
- "What's duct tape that everyone treats as architecture?"
- Listen for: the real event flows vs. the diagrammed ones, identity reconciliation pain, who actually owns which truth.

**CRM/Salesforce team** *(currency: respect for what they've built, protection from blame)*
- "What does your platform get blamed for that actually originates upstream of it?"
- "If the org used what you've already built exactly as intended, what problems would disappear tomorrow?"
- "What's the most painful thing about how other systems feed you?"
- Approach note — held as a hypothesis, not a script: this team *may* have absorbed blame for seams they don't own — or they may be proud, well-credited, and rightly wary of a stranger arriving with sympathy they didn't ask for. Open with questions, read the room, and only name the blame-relocation if their answers put it there first. (This caution applies to every role page in this kit: the currencies and psychologies are probability-weighted guesses to be revised on contact, and a colleague who refuses their assigned part is evidence, not an obstacle.)

**Security & identity** *(currency: reduced attack surface, audit readiness)*
- "Where does access outlive the relationship that justified it?"
- "What would you want from any AI initiative before it touches student data — and who's asked you that question before I did?"
- Listen for: current identity architecture reality, FERPA purpose handling today, appetite for policy-as-code.

**Support & operations leadership** *(currency: fewer repeat contacts, defensible SLAs)*
- "What share of your contacts are students checking status on something they submitted?"
- "Which queues have a named owner per item, and which are owned by 'the team'?"
- "When leadership asks about a stuck case, how long does the answer take to assemble?"

**Fellow architects & engineering leadership** *(currency: being heard, not managed)*
- "What did the last person in my seat get right that nobody credits, and wrong that everybody remembers?"
- "What decision is currently stuck waiting for someone with my title to take the heat for it?"
- "What would make architecture reviews something you'd attend voluntarily?"

## The note template (one page per conversation)

```
WHO / ROLE / DATE:
THEIR WORDS (verbatim gems):
SYSTEMS THEY NAMED (and what each is trusted for):
QUEUES OBSERVED (item→ owner? clock? what happens when it stalls?):
VOCABULARY COLLISIONS (word → what it means HERE):
SEAM EVIDENCE (failure story → ran THROUGH a system / AROUND it / died BETWEEN):
CLASSIFICATION (this person's biggest pain): TECHNICAL / ADAPTIVE — because:
THEIR CURRENCY (what they'd trade for):
WHAT I PROMISED (and by when):
WHAT SURPRISED ME (candidate baseline-belief update):
FOLLOW-UPS:
```

## The two running logs (update after every session)

**The adaptive/technical ledger.** Two columns, every pain point filed into one. Technical column items feed slice candidates. Adaptive column items feed the *relationship* plan — they cannot appear in any roadmap as engineering work, ever. The ratio itself is a finding: if it runs heavily adaptive, Days 31–60 needs more ritual and less diagram.

**The seam-audit register.** Every failure story classified: through / around / between. By Day 30 this register is the evidence base that either proves the seams argument internally or revises it — and either outcome is a win you can present.

## Day 30 outputs this kit must produce

The honest current-state map (systems, owners, queues, seams — in their words). The written diagnosis of top failure modes, quoting the verbatim gems. The vocabulary-collision list, ready for event-storming. The adaptive/technical ledger with its ratio. Your kept-promise record. And a stack of graded baseline statements — see the Day-0 instrument.

*If a conversation produces a page with every field empty except impressions, the kit failed or the listening did. Both are fixable; only one is the kit's fault.*
