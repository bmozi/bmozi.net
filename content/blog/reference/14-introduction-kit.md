# The Introduction Kit — First Words at WGU

*How to arrive as a team player, a servant leader, and one of them — especially among
the other enterprise architects. Principles under load: **Earn Trust** (listen
genuinely, examine your strongest convictions — including the conviction that your
preparation should be visible), **Inspire & Develop** (credit and curiosity before
contribution), **One-by-One** (you join a team one relationship at a time).*

## The one rule above all the scripts

**Servant leadership is never claimed; it is only witnessed.** The moment you say
"I'm a servant leader," you've made the introduction about your self-image. The
moment you ask "what's in your way that I could move?" and then move it, someone
else says it about you — and their sentence is worth a hundred of yours. Every
script below obeys this: no labels for yourself, only behaviors that earn labels
from others. The same goes for the Leadership Principles: never quote them at
colleagues in week one. They live them; citing them back reads as performance.
Live them silently and let people notice.

And the corpus stays holstered. The book, the site, the fabric, the twenty-two
areas — none of it appears in any introduction. Prepared answers arrive only when
real questions create the pull, one page at a time, weeks from now. The strongest
possible signal to fellow architects is a deeply prepared person who leads with
questions anyway — because it means the preparation serves the team, not the ego.

## Script 1 · The thirty-second live intro (team meeting, Day 1 or 2)

> "Hi everyone — I'm John. I've been building systems for about twenty years —
> Intuit, eBay, SoFi, a bunch in between — and I still write code, which I intend
> to keep true here. I took this role because I believe in what this place does:
> my plan for the first month is mostly to listen — to this team first. You know
> things about how this institution really works that I couldn't learn any other
> way, and I'd rather be useful slowly than wrong quickly. I'll be asking each of
> you for time, and the most valuable thing you can give me is the unvarnished
> version. Glad to be here."

Why it works: credentials in one breath (enough to be credible, not enough to
tower); "I still write code" earns engineers; "listen to this team first" and
"unvarnished version" signal that their knowledge outranks your preparation;
"useful slowly rather than wrong quickly" is humility with a spine. Total absence
of: vision, frameworks, the word "transform," and anything that sounds like a plan
they're about to be subjected to.

## Script 2 · The written intro (Slack/Teams/email, week one)

> "Hi all — John Briggs, joining the architecture team. Quick version: ~20 years
> building and running systems (fintech, marketplaces, a few rescues), still
> hands-on, happiest when something I helped design is quietly working and nobody
> has to think about it.
>
> My first-month plan is deliberately boring: 1:1s, listening, and tracing a few
> real student journeys end to end so I understand how things actually flow —
> not how the diagrams say they flow. If you have opinions about what a new
> architect should understand before opening his mouth, I will genuinely take
> that meeting first.
>
> Two standing offers from day one: if something's blocked and an extra pair of
> senior hands would help, I'm available — including for the unglamorous stuff.
> And if you tell me something in confidence, it stays that way. Looking forward
> to meeting everyone properly."

Why it works: "quietly working and nobody has to think about it" is the
anti-showboat identity; "what should I understand before opening my mouth" makes
their expertise the door you knock on; the unglamorous-work offer is servant
leadership shown, not said; the confidence promise starts the trust ledger
immediately.

## Script 3 · The 1:1 opener with each fellow architect (the delicate one)

Open with THEIR work, stay there for most of the meeting:

> "Before anything about me — what are you carrying right now? What's the piece
> of architecture here you're proudest of, and what's the one you'd redo if
> anyone would let you?"

Then the three questions from the field kit, in this order, because each earns the
next: *"What did the last person in my seat get right that nobody credits — and
wrong that everybody remembers?"* (tells you the local failure modes AND signals
you intend to learn from their history, not repeat it). *"What decision is
currently stuck waiting for someone with my title to take the heat?"* (offers your
political capital in service of THEIR backlog — the single fastest "one of us"
move available to a new peer). And last, always last: *"What's in your way that
someone like me could move?"* Then move one thing within the week and report back.

Close by asking, not telling: *"How do you like to work with other architects —
what did good collaboration look like here when it happened?"* You will learn more
about the team's real culture from that answer than from any onboarding deck.

## The peer-architect playbook (beyond the first meeting)

**Defend their work in rooms they're not in.** The first time another architect
hears secondhand that you praised their design to leadership — accurately, not as
flattery — you become one of them. This outperforms every direct gesture, because
it's the one they know wasn't performed for them.

**Ask one of them to teach you something real in week one.** Not politeness —
an actual working session on a system they own. Being teachable is the fastest
credibility with experts; every expert has been condescended to by a new arrival,
and you will be the one who wasn't that.

**Take the unglamorous item off the shared plate.** The stale documentation, the
review nobody wants, the meeting nobody wants to run. Once, visibly, without
being asked twice. Team-player status is purchased in exactly this currency and
no other.

**Disagree in private first, forever.** The charter's no-surprise-vetoes rule
matters most with peers: the first time you have a concern about an architect's
design, they hear it from you, alone, early — and when it surfaces in a group,
you present their counterargument fairly. One cycle of that and your dissent
becomes welcome instead of feared.

**Let them find the corpus.** Someone will eventually ask "have you written about
this?" — the only right moment for any of it to appear, and even then: one page,
offered, never assigned.

## What NOT to do in week one — the anti-checklist

Don't present anything. Don't propose anything. Don't correct anything — even
things that are wrong; write them in the field notes and let the Days 31–60
sessions surface them with the team as co-authors. Don't mention the book, the
site, or the fabric unsolicited. Don't say "servant leader," "thought leader,"
"transformation," or "best practices." Don't quote Leadership Principles at
people who live them. And don't fill silences — the pauses in your 1:1s are where
the unvarnished versions live.

---

*The measure of a successful introduction is embarrassingly simple: at the end of
week two, do the other architects talk to you when they don't have to? Everything
above is engineered toward that single observable — because "one of them" isn't a
status you announce. It's a lunch you get invited to.*
