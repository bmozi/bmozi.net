# Build the Guild — The Second-Quarter Project

*The plan for becoming a leader of architects, not just an architect who leads. Target window: Q2 of tenure (roughly Oct 18 – Jan 17), gated on preconditions — a guild started before its preconditions is a book club with a fancy name. Principles under load: **Inspire & Develop** ("we believe everyone is a leader" is this project's entire thesis), **One-by-One** (architects grow the same way students do — individually, by demonstrated mastery), **Earn Trust** and **Align & Commit** throughout.*

---

## What the guild is, and pointedly is not

The guild is an *enabling structure*: a community of practice that raises the architectural judgment of everyone who touches design decisions — architects by title, senior engineers by inclination, and the "accidental architects" every organization is full of. It teaches, reviews, and stewards shared standards.

It is **not** a review board (no gates — the operating model already forbids them), **not** a shadow org chart, **not** a channel for me to scale my opinions (the research is blunt: architects talking mainly to architects is how EA practices quietly die), and **not** mandatory. Attendance earned by usefulness is the only attendance worth having — the paved-road rule, applied to people.

The founding measure, adopted from the failure literature and worth stating at the first meeting: **the guild exists to make itself progressively less necessary.** Its score is the Niemi count — how much architectural judgment keeps operating when no architect is in the room.

## Preconditions (the gate on starting — check at Day ~90)

1. **Slice 1 shipped and measured.** The guild's credibility rides on the lighthouse: teaching from a delivered win lands; teaching from slideware breeds the ivory tower with extra steps.
2. **The self-identified core exists.** Baseline statement D3 predicted 2+ future guild members would reveal themselves unprompted in month one. The guild starts with the people who leaned in — never with a conscription list.
3. **The team-ratified standards exist.** Days 31–60 produced team-owned principles and an ADR practice; the guild stewards *those*, not my private canon. Stewarding what the team already owns is Align & Commit; importing my hub wholesale is colonization.
4. **My own loops are proven.** I don't teach the decision journal, the premortem, or calibration until my Monday check-in record shows I've *lived* them for a quarter (baseline E4). Credibility of practice, not of position.

If any precondition is missing at Day 90, the guild slips — and that slip is announced openly, with the reason. Modeling "sequence over calendar" is itself a lesson.

## The four rituals (the guild IS its rituals)

**1 · The Design Clinic (weekly, 50 minutes, standing).**
Any team brings any seam problem; the room works it live. Rules: the bringer states the problem in domain language first (no solutions for ten minutes); every option surfaced gets its cost named ("what does this cost us?" institutionalized); the clinic *advises* — the bringer decides and owns. Architecture attends every time and decides only when asked. This is the single highest-leverage ritual: it moves review from gate to gift, and every clinic is invisible ShadowBox training for everyone watching how seniors reason.

**2 · Kata Night (monthly, 90 minutes, voluntary and fed).**
Small teams, an RFP-style scenario (start from the public kata catalogs, then graduate to sanitized versions of our own real decisions — including, eventually, prosecuting decisions *I* made, with me in the room). Teams must produce not just a design but its ADR with trade-offs and its one-page prosecution. The debrief compares against what the seniors saw — expert-comparison practice, which is the only training method for tacit judgment with real evidence behind it.

**3 · The Crash-Report Club (monthly, 45 minutes).**
One documented industry failure per session — an ERP disaster, a disbanded EA practice, a migration post-mortem — analyzed *before* reading the conclusions: "what would we have flagged?" Then the reveal, and the gap between our judgment and the record is the lesson. Pilots study crashes; so do we. Sector cases (the five-year-median SIS migrations) get priority — they're our aircraft.

**4 · ADR Rounds (rotating, async-first).**
Every significant ADR gets a reviewer from *outside* the authoring team, drawn from the guild — junior members explicitly included, because reviewing a principal's ADR with a real voice is the fastest development experience we can manufacture. The rotation is the mentorship program wearing work clothes: no coffee-chat pairings, just real decisions with real names on them.

## The teaching spine (reuse, don't rewrite)

The material already exists in four altitudes; the guild gets the internal editions.
The fourth is *Harnessing the Horse*: as AI-assisted engineering becomes every team's
daily reality, the book's Harness Framework (Scope, Prove, Enforce, Communicate)
becomes the guild's agentic-engineering track — the manuscript's exercises and the
companion repo's checklists, prompts, and anti-pattern catalog are ready-made Kata
Night material, and the delegation filter (what to hand the agent vs. what judgment
keeps) pairs naturally with the trust pack's action tiers: the same governance
instinct at personal and institutional scale. One sequencing note: this track ships
in its ten-minute brief form first (see the talk kit) and goes deep only where teams
pull — a book-length track nobody asked for is the ivory tower with a reading list. *Seams for Everyone* becomes the shared-vocabulary track for partners outside engineering — registrar's office, enrollment, support leadership are invited to those sessions explicitly (a guild that only architects attend is the erosion pattern with snacks). *Getting It Right* becomes the practitioner study track, one pattern per month, paired with the reference implementation as lab material — read it, then break it, then fix it. *The Architect's Mind* seeds the senior circle — quarterly evening sessions for the handful carrying decision weight, where the material is judgment and the format is honest war stories against the essays. And the glossary is the guild's first shared artifact to *own and edit* — transferring stewardship of the vocabulary is the first act of making the guild theirs, not mine.

## Growth architecture (One-by-One, for architects)

Competency-based, naturally: a lightweight guild ladder defined by *demonstrated evidence*, never tenure — first authored ADR that survives rounds; first clinic problem brought and resolved; first kata led; first crash-report session hosted; first prosecution written against a design you loved. Each is observable, each is portable ("I ran design clinics" means something anywhere), and the ladder doubles as the succession plan: by the end of Q3, every ritual has a non-me host who has run it at least twice. The charter's tenth commitment — designing for my own absence — has a schedule now.

## Metrics (numbers with consequences, applied to ourselves)

The Niemi count, quarterly: artifacts, rituals, and decisions that operated with no architect present — the guild's true north, and it must grow. ADRs authored by non-architects (target: majority by end of Q3). Clinic demand (problems brought voluntarily per month — if it drops, the clinic got preachy; fix the clinic, don't summon the audience). Time-to-design-feedback for any team that asks (the review-as-service SLA). And one integrity-style number: zero rituals that have become status meetings — any ritual that stops producing changed decisions gets retired at the quarterly review, publicly, as a demonstration that we prune ourselves.

## Risks, named now (the guild's own premortem)

**Ivory Tower 2.0** — the guild becomes architects performing for architects. Tripwire: two consecutive clinics with no non-architect in the room. Response: the partner tracks get priority over the practitioner tracks for a month.
**The gate reflex** — ADR rounds quietly harden into approval. Tripwire: anyone *waiting* on the guild to proceed. Response: restate advise-don't-decide in writing; apologize to the team that waited.
**Mandatory fun** — leadership loves it and makes it compulsory. Response: decline the mandate explicitly; attendance data stays out of anyone's performance review, stated in the guild charter.
**Founder gravity** — it orbits me. Tripwire: any ritual that has never run without me by end of Q2. Response: hand off the healthiest ritual first, not the weakest.
**Premature start** — the Day-90 precondition check fails but enthusiasm (mine) pushes ahead anyway. Response: the preconditions are published; hold me to them. This document is the witness.

## The thirteen-week sketch (adjust to reality, keep the sequence)

Weeks 1–2: found the core — the self-identified few plus invitations earned in Q1; draft the one-page guild charter *together* (their words, my scaffold). Weeks 3–4: first Design Clinic, seeded with a real Slice-2 problem; glossary stewardship handed over. Weeks 5–6: first Kata Night (public scenario); partner track opens with *Seams for Everyone* session one for enrollment and records folks. Weeks 7–8: first Crash-Report Club (an SIS migration case); ADR rounds begin on live Slice-2 ADRs. Weeks 9–10: first non-me clinic host; senior circle convenes once. Weeks 11–13: first internal kata from a sanitized real decision; quarterly review of guild metrics *by the guild*; Q3 plan drafted by someone who isn't me.
