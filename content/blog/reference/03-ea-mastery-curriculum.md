# The EA Mastery Curriculum

*A competency-based program for becoming the best enterprise architect this institution and its students could have. Mastery is demonstrated by evidence, never by seat time — the same standard the institution holds for its students is the standard here.*

---

## The organizing truth

Thirty years of enterprise-architecture evidence reduces to one adversarial finding, and this entire curriculum is built on it:

**Enterprise architecture fails when it optimizes for architecture, and succeeds when it optimizes for other people's decisions.**

The failure literature is better validated than any success formula: architects get disbanded for ivory-tower drift, models-as-product, governance-as-tollbooth, value deferred beyond stakeholders' planning cycles, and technology framing at tables that speak money and mission. The research's sharpest diagnostic, worth adopting as a standing self-audit, is Niemi's six-month test: *if the architecture team stopped attending meetings for six months, what architectural understanding would remain in the organization?* If the answer is "little," the practice is already dying — it just hasn't been told yet.

So the curriculum inverts the certification industry. Frameworks appear here only as vocabulary. What gets trained is what the evidence says distinguishes the indispensable: decision quality, influence, financial fluency, maintained technical credibility, boundary judgment, and — the meta-skill — manufacturing feedback in a profession that withholds it.

## Domain 1 · Decision Quality Under Uncertainty

**Mastery means:** your architectural judgments are measurably better calibrated than they were a year ago, and every significant decision leaves a record that lets reality grade it.

**Why it's first:** architecture's core handicap is that feedback on decisions arrives years late and confounded. The only empirically supported response is to engineer shorter, less-distorted feedback loops — this domain is that engineering, applied to yourself.

**The practice regimen (evidence-backed):**
- **The decision journal, as ADRs.** Every significant decision recorded before the outcome: context, options, choice, reasoning, and — critically — *what you expect to happen and by when.* Hindsight bias is among the most replicated effects in cognitive psychology; a written pre-outcome record is the only defense. You already run an ADR practice; this adds the predictive clause that turns it into a calibration instrument.
- **Probability-tagged predictions.** Attach explicit probabilities to your professional judgments — "70% this integration ships by March," "90% this vendor claim survives testing" — logged, then scored when resolved (Brier scoring). The Tetlock research shows brief calibration training produces lasting accuracy gains; architects who practice this become the person in the room whose "I think it's likely" actually means something.
- **Premortems on every major design.** Before commitment: "It's eighteen months later and this failed — write the story." The prospective-hindsight mechanism increases distinct failure reasons generated by roughly 30%, and it's the cheapest robust technique in the entire literature. Your adversarial-review practice is the premortem's big sibling; the premortem is its fast, per-decision form.
- **Failure-case study as expert comparison.** Monthly: one documented architecture or transformation failure (ERP disasters, EA-team disbandings, migration post-mortems), analyzed *before reading the conclusions* — what would I have flagged? — then compared against what the record shows. This approximates ShadowBox training, the closest thing to a validated method for transmitting expert tacit judgment.

**Evidence of mastery:** a year of ADRs with predictive clauses; a prediction log with a falling Brier score; premortem records attached to major designs; a folder of failure-case analyses with your pre-read judgments visible.

**Adversarial self-audit:** pull your five most confident positions from a year ago. How many survived contact with reality? If all five, you're either brilliant or not writing down the real positions.

## Domain 2 · Influence Without Authority

**Mastery means:** the organization changes direction because of your reasoning, in rooms you're not in, through people who don't report to you.

**Why it matters most here:** a university is the purest test of this skill that exists — shared governance, genuinely autonomous units, and colleagues who can simply ignore you. Command-and-control architecture that merely annoys a corporation is dead on arrival in higher education. This is a feature: the environment forces the only kind of influence that's real.

**The practice regimen:**
- **Exchange thinking (Cohen & Bradford).** Influence is trading in currencies others value: making their decisions easier, making their risks visible early, making their work look good to their leadership. Before every ask, name the currency you're offering. Your charter's "serve before I ask" is this, formalized — practice it as deliberately as a technique, not just a value.
- **Adaptive-vs-technical diagnosis (Heifetz).** The classic transformation-killer is applying a technical fix to an adaptive challenge — producing a target-state model when the real problem is getting an organization to change how it decides. Before every initiative, classify it: does this need engineering, or does it need people to let go of something? Almost every failed EA program in the literature misdiagnosed this exactly once, fatally.
- **Servant-leadership behaviors, deliberately.** The one leadership style with meta-analytic support (incremental validity over transformational and authentic leadership, mediated by trust): visible removal of others' obstacles, credit distribution by name, development of the people around you as a measured activity. Not a personality — a practiced behavior set.
- **The being-reliably-right ledger.** Your most durable influence currency is being right in public, verifiably, over time — which is Domain 1's prediction log doing double duty. When your calibrated track record is visible, "John thinks this is risky" starts moving budgets.

**Evidence of mastery:** decisions that went your way in meetings you didn't attend; teams adopting the paved road without mandate; being asked for counsel on problems outside your lane; the seam audit or glossary being cited by people who don't know you wrote them.

**Adversarial self-audit:** Niemi's six-month test, personalized — if you went silent for six months, what of your thinking would keep operating? Count the artifacts, rituals, and people. That count is your real influence; the rest is attendance.

## Domain 3 · Financial & Institutional Fluency

**Mastery means:** you can hold the provost's and CFO's conversation in their language, and your architecture arguments arrive pre-translated into money and mission.

**Why the evidence demands it:** financial modeling is the single scarcest EA competency in current industry research, and executive-table testimony converges on the same point — nobody at that table asks about framework phases; they ask what it costs, what it returns, what it risks, and whom it serves.

**The study program:**
- **Unit economics as a first language.** Cost per student per term, cost per case resolved, cost per point of persistence — you've built this muscle in the business case; now make it reflexive for every proposal, yours and others'.
- **Options framing.** An architecture that defers a decision has quantifiable value (the deferral is an option); an architecture that forecloses choices has a quantifiable cost. This is the most persuasive financial frame for architectural investment ever devised — learn to price flexibility.
- **Higher-ed finance literacy.** Net tuition revenue, enrollment funnel economics, discount rates, outcome-based funding, research overhead, accreditation cost-of-failure. The institution's planning conversations run on these; an architect fluent in them gets invited back.
- **The mission ledger.** This institution counts lives changed. Every financial argument you make should carry its mission line — not as decoration, but because at this particular table, mission math genuinely breaks ties.

**Evidence of mastery:** a business case that survived finance review without translation help; being asked by non-IT leadership to pressure-test *their* numbers; architecture options priced in dollars and student outcomes side by side.

**Adversarial self-audit:** explain your current top initiative in three sentences containing no technology nouns. If you can't, you don't understand it at the executive altitude yet.

## Domain 4 · Technical Credibility, Maintained

**Mastery means:** engineers check their designs with you because you make them better, and you can still build the thing you're asking others to build.

**Why the evidence is unambiguous:** "architects shouldn't code" is folklore, contradicted by every serious competency source — the exemplary-practitioner tenet, the staff-engineer literature, and the failure data (ivory towers are staffed by people who stopped touching the work). Credibility in the engine room is what makes the penthouse listen.

**The practice regimen:**
- **The elevator ride, scheduled.** Deliberately alternate altitudes: strategy work and hands-on work in the same week, every week. The reference implementation was this — keep that muscle: one running artifact per quarter that demonstrates a pattern you're advocating.
- **Evaluative depth over breadth.** The goal isn't knowing every technology; it's the depth to interrogate any vendor or team claim — about consistency, replication, latency, cost — and detect when the answer is marketing (Kleppmann-grade reading, applied).
- **Review as craft.** Design reviews where your questions teach: not "why didn't you use X" but "what happens when this input arrives twice?" The quality of your questions is the visible tip of your technical iceberg, and it's what engineers gossip about.

**Evidence of mastery:** engineers voluntarily bringing you designs early; your quarterly artifact repos; a review question of yours that changed a design, retold by someone else.

**Adversarial self-audit:** when did an engineer last correct you on a technical point — and did they learn that correcting you is rewarded? If it's been months, you've either ossified or become unsafe to correct. Both are failures.

## Domain 5 · Boundary & Seam Judgment

**Mastery means:** you consistently put system, team, and vendor boundaries where the organization's fault lines actually are — the skill your entire architecture thesis rests on.

**The study program:**
- **Strategic DDD as the core discipline.** Bounded contexts, context mapping, ubiquitous language — the most durable boundary tool the field has produced. Study the strategic half deeply (Khononov is the modern entry; Evans for the source); hold the tactical half loosely (it overengineers simple domains).
- **Conway's law as a design instrument.** Team boundaries and system boundaries designed as one act; the inverse maneuver when needed; the wisdom to know when the org shape should win.
- **Sector reference models.** HERM (the higher-ed capability reference model) is more used in practice than any generic framework content — learn it well enough to map your capability model to it, so your boundaries speak the sector's shared language.
- **Federated governance as the default.** In this sector, standards over mandates, partnership over control — always. The operating model you've already designed is the right shape; the mastery is holding it under pressure to centralize.

**Evidence of mastery:** bounded contexts that survive two years without a redraw; a vendor exit executed through an adapter as designed; teams that stopped fighting because a boundary moved.

**Adversarial self-audit:** which of your current boundaries exists because of a real fault line, and which because of who attended the meeting? Name one of each, honestly.

## Domain 6 · Feedback Manufacturing (the meta-domain)

**Mastery means:** you have built, for yourself and your organization, the feedback loops the profession naturally lacks — so learning compounds instead of accumulating as anecdote.

**The system (this is the curriculum's engine):**
- **Daily**, ~5 minutes: any significant judgment made → one line in the decision journal, probability attached if resolvable.
- **Weekly**, in the Monday check-in: resolve any predictions that closed; one influence observation (what moved without your push?); one adaptive-vs-technical classification for the week's main challenge.
- **Monthly**, ~2 hours: one failure case studied ShadowBox-style; one kata or design exercise with a colleague where you defend under challenge; Brier score reviewed.
- **Quarterly**, a half day: the self-prosecution — run the fired-EA behavior checklist against your own quarter (models-as-product? governance-as-gate? value invisible on others' timescales? one-floor residence?); run the six-month test; publish what you found to yourself in writing; ship the quarter's hands-on artifact.
- **Annually:** re-run the adversarial review of your own architecture *and* of this curriculum. Both are designs. Both decay.

**Evidence of mastery:** the loops exist, run without willpower, and have visibly changed at least one position you held — with the change recorded.

## The twelve-month arc

**Months 1–3 (the 90-day plan, already dated):** Domains 2 and 6 lead — listening is influence-gathering, and the journal/prediction habits start now, while stakes are low and baselines honest. Read: Hohpe's *Architect Elevator* (the role), Khononov (boundaries, before the Days 31–60 sessions).

**Months 4–6 (first slices ship):** Domain 1 gets its first real grades — slice predictions resolve. Domain 3 study begins in earnest: the internal financial model replaces public math, and you learn the institution's real planning language. Read: *Accelerate* + current DORA (with its construct-validity caveats), higher-ed finance primers.

**Months 7–9 (expansion earns or stalls):** Domain 5 under load — boundaries meet reality; the operating model gets ratified or revised. First quarterly artifacts have shipped; the review-as-craft reputation is forming. Read: Kleppmann 2nd ed. (evaluative depth), Team Topologies (held loosely, as lens not template).

**Months 10–12 (the leader emerges):** Domain 2 at full strength — the mentoring, the guild, the succession thinking. Run the annual self-prosecution. Decide, from evidence, what year two optimizes. Read: the leadership shelf critically (Marquet for intent-based language, servant-leadership sources for the one style with data), and one thing from far outside the field.

## The standing failure checklist (quarterly, forever)

The fired-EA behaviors, as questions — because the failure modes are better validated than any success formula:

1. Did I produce any model this quarter whose primary consumer was other architects?
2. Did any team experience my involvement as a gate rather than a service?
3. Can every major stakeholder name something architecture did for them *this quarter*?
4. Have I spent real hours in both the penthouse and the engine room this month?
5. Did I frame any argument in technology terms at a table that speaks money and mission?
6. If I went silent for six months, what would keep running? (Count it. Write it down. Grow the count.)

*This curriculum is itself held to the charter: falsifiable, revised in the open, prosecuted annually. The goal was never to be impressive. It's the same goal the institution has for every student — demonstrated mastery, in service of the person on the other end of the screen.*
