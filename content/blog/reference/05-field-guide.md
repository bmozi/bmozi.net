# The Field Guide — Every Idea in One Breath, Applied This Week, Principled

*Companion to the Mastery Curriculum and Reading Canon. Every entry carries three parts: the idea explained plainly (the breath), the concrete application in your actual chair at WGU this week (the practice), and the Leadership Principles it exercises with the reason why (the alignment). Per the Editorial North Star's first check: a teaching that can't name its principles isn't ready — and that rule applies to this guide most of all.*

---

## The spine

**The Architect Elevator (Hohpe)**
*The breath:* A building where executives live on the top floor and engineers work in the basement, and they've never met. Your job isn't to live on either floor — it's to be the elevator, carrying the basement's truth up and the penthouse's intent down, translating at every stop.
*This week at WGU:* Look at your calendar. If it's all one altitude — all leadership meetings or all technical sessions — you've parked the elevator. During Days 1–30, your 1:1 list *is* the elevator route: registrar and provost-level conversations in the same weeks as engineer walkthroughs of actual integration code.
*Principles exercised:* **Align & Commit** — the elevator is synchronizing work with the right roles, made physical. **Earn Trust** — each floor trusts you because you speak its language honestly, not a translation that flatters.

**Strategic Domain-Driven Design (Khononov, Evans)**
*The breath:* The same word means different things in different rooms. "Enrolled" means one thing to the registrar, another to financial aid, another to marketing. Instead of forcing one dictionary on everyone, draw a line around each room, keep each room's language pure inside its line, and put translators at the doors.
*This week at WGU:* In every Days 1–30 conversation, collect the collisions — words that mean different things across departments. Each collision is a bounded-context boundary announcing itself. Bring the collision list to the Days 31–60 event-storming sessions; it's better fuel than any diagram.
*Principles exercised:* **Learning** — listening to understand each room's language before proposing anything. **Align & Commit** — boundaries and translators are how interdependent teams work without grinding against each other.

**Accelerate / DORA**
*The breath:* Someone finally measured thousands of software teams and found the few habits that actually predict success — small pieces, fast feedback, easy recovery. Not genius; habits. And the newest finding: AI amplifies what a team already is — it makes good teams better and struggling teams worse.
*This week at WGU:* When anyone proposes AI as the fix for a struggling process, you have the evidence-backed reply: amplifiers amplify what's there — fix the basics, then add AI. And quote DORA *with* its caveats; colleagues notice who cites evidence honestly.
*Principles exercised:* **Sound Judgment** — data-driven insight, including about the data's own limits. **Integrity** — quoting the caveats when it would be easier not to is doing right when no one would catch you.

**Designing Data-Intensive Applications (Kleppmann & Riccomini)**
*The breath:* Every vendor makes promises — "always consistent," "never loses data." This book teaches what's physically possible, so you can tell which promises are real, which have fine print, and which are marketing.
*This week at WGU:* In your next design review or vendor conversation, ask one Kleppmann-grade question: "What happens to in-flight writes during a partition?" You don't need a gotcha — you need the room to learn that claims get interrogated here.
*Principles exercised:* **Learning** — benchmarking claims against the best available knowledge, insatiably. **Deliver Results** — students are served by systems that keep promises, and promises get kept only when someone checked the physics first.

## The lenses

**Team Topologies (Skelton & Pais)**
*The breath:* Teams can only hold so much in their heads — like a backpack that overflows. And systems end up shaped like the teams that build them, whether you plan it or not. So plan it: design team shapes and system shapes as one decision.
*This week at WGU:* In inventory conversations, listen for overflowing backpacks — teams owning so many systems they can't do any well. Those observations justify the operating model in Days 31–60 far better than the book's vocabulary will. Cite the pain, not the framework.
*Principles exercised:* **Align & Commit** — Conway's law is interdependency thinking with teeth. **Inspire & Develop** — respecting cognitive load is how you set every team up to do its best work.

**Wardley Mapping**
*The breath:* Everything you build sits somewhere on a journey from brand-new invention to boring commodity like electricity. The map shows where each piece sits, so you stop custom-building commodities and stop buying what should be your invention.
*This week at WGU:* ADR-001 already is this argument — sketch the one-axis version when you present it: identity resolution near the commodity end, the competency ledger at genesis. Executives grasp it in seconds. But remember the map persuades beyond its evidence — use it to show reasoning, never replace it.
*Principles exercised:* **Imagine Boldly** — placing the ledger at genesis is claiming where WGU should be first in the world. **Create & Innovate** — leverage the best of the commodity past, invent only where the future is unserved.

**Fundamentals / The Hard Parts (Richards & Ford)**
*The breath:* There are no right answers in architecture, only trade-offs — every good thing you pick costs some other good thing. The skill isn't finding the option without downsides; it's naming the downsides out loud and choosing them on purpose.
*This week at WGU:* Institute "what does this cost us?" in every design conversation — including about your own proposals. The architect who names his own design's costs first becomes the most trusted person in the room within a month.
*Principles exercised:* **Sound Judgment** — owning trade-offs is the mechanics of making right choices a lot. **Earn Trust** — examining your strongest convictions in public, one design at a time.

**Residuality Theory (O'Reilly)**
*The breath:* You can't predict what will attack your design, so don't predict — bombard. Throw twenty random disasters at it on paper ("vendor triples prices," "enrollment doubles") and keep whatever survives most of them.
*This week at WGU:* Steal the bombardment as a premortem variant for the Slice 1 review: ten minutes, everyone writes random stressors on cards, the design gets stress-walked. Skip the theory; keep the exercise.
*Principles exercised:* **Create & Innovate** — fresh thinking from anywhere, even contested theory, mined for the useful part. **Learning** — inviting the whole room to attack the design is inquiry made structural.

## The leadership shelf

**Influence Without Authority (Cohen & Bradford)**
*The breath:* You can't order anyone around, so influence works like trading. Everyone values something — looking good to their boss, less risk, saved time. Offer what they value genuinely, and your ask stops being a favor and becomes an exchange.
*This week at WGU:* Before each 1:1, write one line: "What currency does this person trade in?" The registrar trades in *record integrity and risk reduction*. Mentors trade in *time back for students*. Engineering leads trade in *fewer 2am pages*. Your architecture offers all three — present each person theirs.
*Principles exercised:* **Earn Trust** — genuine exchange is listening genuinely, then doing your job so well it pays others. **Student Obsessed** — every currency above cashes out, one hop later, in a student served better.

**Adaptive vs. Technical (Heifetz)**
*The breath:* Some problems are broken machines — an expert can fix them. Others are people-need-to-change problems — no expert can, only the people themselves, and it's uncomfortable, and they'd rather you hand them a machine. The deadliest leadership mistake: handing over a machine when the real problem is the changing.
*This week at WGU:* Two columns for everything you hear in Days 1–30. "Documents lack status tracking" — technical; build it. "Departments don't trust each other's data" — adaptive; only shared ownership rituals fix it, slowly. Your slice strategy is secretly this: Slice 1 is deliberately technical and *creates the trust* that funds the adaptive work.
*Principles exercised:* **Sound Judgment** — the classification *is* judgment; misdiagnosis is how transformations die. **Aim True** — adaptive work is stewardship: patient, mission-anchored, thinking in years.

**Servant Leadership (the meta-analyses)**
*The breath:* The leader whose team performs best is usually the one asking "what's blocking you, and how do I remove it?" — not the one giving speeches. Measured across hundreds of studies, it keeps beating the fancier-sounding styles.
*This week at WGU:* End every 1:1 with "What's in your way that someone like me could move?" Then move one thing and report back. Two weeks of this buys more organizational capital than a quarter of presentations.
*Principles exercised:* **Inspire & Develop** — removing obstacles is how you expand others' ability to lead. **One-by-One** — you serve the organization one unblocked person at a time, exactly as WGU serves one student at a time.

**Turn the Ship Around (Marquet)**
*The breath:* A submarine captain stopped giving orders and had his crew say "I intend to..." instead — forcing them to think like owners while he kept the veto. Competence rose because thinking moved to where the information was.
*This week at WGU:* Use "I intend to" upward first: "I intend to bring the timeline product as a hypothesis to the ratification sessions" — informs your leadership while giving them the veto. Later, invite it from the architects around you.
*Principles exercised:* **Inspire & Develop** — "we believe everyone is a leader" is this book's entire thesis in WGU's own words. **Align & Commit** — intent-based language respects the decision owner explicitly, every time.

**Superforecasting (Tetlock)**
*The breath:* Some people are measurably better at predicting the future — not smarter, just scorekeepers. They say "70% likely" instead of "probably," write it down, and check later. Keeping score is the entire trick, and it's learnable.
*This week at WGU:* Start the log with three real entries today: "80% the seam audit finds a failure that ran *around* the CRM." "60% the current SIS estate differs from the public record." Check them at Day 30 — Monday's check-in will ask.
*Principles exercised:* **Sound Judgment** — calibration is judgment, measured. **Integrity** — logging your misses where you can't unremember them is honesty with no witness required.

**Premortems (Klein)**
*The breath:* Instead of asking "what could go wrong?" (everyone shrugs), announce "it's next year and this failed completely — write the story of why." Pretending the disaster already happened unlocks about a third more honesty from the same brains.
*This week at WGU:* Run one on your own 90-day plan, tonight, alone: "It's October 18th and the first 90 days failed." Whatever you write is your real risk register — compare it against the official one.
*Principles exercised:* **Sound Judgment** — the cheapest robust decision-quality technique in the literature. **Learning** — learning from mistakes *before* making them is the principle at its most efficient.

## The sector shelf

**HERM (the higher-ed capability model)**
*The breath:* A shared map of everything a university does — admissions, teaching, records, research — used across hundreds of institutions, so architects can compare notes without translating first.
*This week at WGU:* Map your eight bounded contexts to HERM (an afternoon). Your boundary map now speaks the sector's language — which matters the day you present to anyone who's attended an EDUCAUSE conference.
*Principles exercised:* **Learning** — benchmarking against the best includes speaking the community's shared language. **Align & Commit** — adopting the sector standard instead of inventing a rival one is commitment to something larger than your own vocabulary.

**The disbanded-EA-practice case (EDUCAUSE)**
*The breath:* A university built an ambitious architecture practice; three years later the CIO shut it down — not because architecture was wrong, but because the practice was built bigger than the institution was ready to support.
*This week at WGU:* Let this case set your Days 31–60 scoping. The operating model the institution can sustain *this year* beats the ideal one it can't. Every slice expansion earned by evidence is an expansion the institution has proven itself ready for.
*Principles exercised:* **Aim True** — stewardship of the organization means sizing ambition to its real capacity. **Urgency** — speed *relative to risk*: the fastest sustainable pace, not the fastest imaginable one.

**The ERP/SIS failure literature**
*The breath:* When universities replace their big record systems, median disruption is five years, and the projects that die usually die from scope growing mid-flight. These are the crash reports for the aircraft you may fly.
*This week at WGU:* Before any records-workstream conversation, re-read one case. Hold the line you've drawn: cohort cutover, drain don't migrate, per-domain authority transfer. When someone proposes adding "one more system" to a migration, the five-year median is your one-number reply.
*Principles exercised:* **One-by-One** — cohort cutover exists so no individual student is ever stranded by a migration. **Sound Judgment** — pilots who study crash reports fly differently; so do architects.

## How this fits — and the rule it obeys

The **curriculum** is rigor (domains, loops, evidence of mastery). The **canon** is judgment (what to believe, how much). This **field guide** is reflex — the breath, the Monday action, and the principles, because in this practice a lesson that can't name its principles isn't finished. Notice, reading back through, that four principles carry most of the load — Sound Judgment, Earn Trust, Learning, Align & Commit — and that's not an accident: they're the four that govern how an architect *thinks, relates, grows, and moves*. The others arrive through the work itself. When you find an entry whose principle line feels forced, that's a signal worth trusting: either the teaching doesn't belong here, or I tagged it lazily. Prosecute it at the annual review like everything else.

*Revised annually, prosecuted with the rest.*
