# The Interpreter at the Border

*Part 7 of Seams for Everyone — big system ideas, explained with things you already understand. Views my own.*

---

Two delegations face each other across a long table at a border crossing, and between them sits one person with no goods to sell: the interpreter.

The merchants on the left don't speak a word of the language on the right. They never will — and here's the remarkable part — they never *need* to. Grain moves one way, cloth moves the other, contracts get honored for decades. Because the interpreter does something subtler than swapping words. She translates *at the boundary*, and inside that job is a quiet act of protection.

The other side measures cloth in a unit her merchants have never heard of? She converts it before it reaches their ears. The other side's contracts open with three paragraphs of ceremonial formality? She renders the meaning and lets the ceremony stay at the table. Their calendar starts the year in spring? Inside her merchants' ledgers, it's still their own calendar. Everything foreign gets exchanged, at the border, into the language her side already thinks in.

So the two countries trade for a century, and this is the miracle worth italics: *neither one becomes the other.* Each keeps its own language, units, calendar, ways. The strangeness of the neighbor stops at the table — carried across as meaning, never as quirks.

Now imagine the tempting shortcut. The interpreter costs money, and the neighbor is friendly — so skip her. Have your merchants learn the neighbor's tongue. Keep the ledgers in the neighbor's units, since that's how the goods arrive anyway. Adopt their calendar for the contracts. Each step is individually sensible.

Come back in ten years. Your clerks now *think* in the neighbor's units. Your ledgers are structured around their calendar. Your apprentices learn the neighbor's language before they learn your ledger system, because that's what the ledgers demand. And when a better trading partner appears across the other border — better cloth, better prices — you discover the real price of the shortcut: you can't switch. Not because of any contract, but because the neighbor's language is *load-bearing* in your own house. Their quirks are in your walls.

Nobody conquered you. You just skipped the interpreter, and osmosis did the rest.

## In your organization…

Every vendor system you buy speaks its own language — its own names for things, its own codes, its own odd shapes of data. That's fine; it's theirs. The question is where the translation happens.

With an interpreter at the border, the vendor's language stops at the connection point: everything gets converted into *your* company's terms before it flows inward, and your teams, reports, and other systems never learn the vendor exists. Without one, the vendor's field names creep into your reports, their codes into your training manuals, their quirks into every system that touches theirs — until the day you want to switch vendors and discover the switch means renovating your own house. That's not a licensing fee. That's the interpreter's salary, paid retroactively, at hostage rates.

## Engineers call this…

…an **adapter**, or in its most disciplined form an **anti-corruption layer** — a thin translation layer at the boundary whose entire job is making sure outside systems never dictate your internal language. When your engineers ask for time to "build an abstraction over the vendor" instead of wiring it straight in, they can look like they're gold-plating. They're not. They're hiring the interpreter — so that a century from now, you're still trading on your own terms, in your own tongue, free to change neighbors.

Which vendor's vocabulary has quietly become your company's native language?

That's the whole idea. The rest is engineering.
